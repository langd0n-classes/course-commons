# Writing Voice Guide

**Status: CANONICAL.**

This file defines the default writing voice for course materials and lists AI
writing signals to avoid.

Apply these rules to lecture guides, notebooks, syllabus language,
student-facing documents, assignment copy, and planning notes that will be
shared with others.

---

## 1. Voice

Course content should sound like a knowledgeable instructor speaking to a
small group of capable students.

It should be direct, specific, and honest about difficulty.

It should not sound like a textbook, a consultant, or a chatbot.

Prefer:

- direct over polished
- specific over general
- active over passive
- honest over soothing

Examples:

- `Run this cell.`
- `This part is hard.`
- `Check the schema before you merge.`

Less useful:

- `This step may present some challenges.`
- `The relevant information should be reviewed.`

---

## 2. Banned Or Suspicious Phrases

Avoid phrases that strongly signal unreviewed AI output:

- `it's worth noting`
- `it is important to note`
- `in conclusion`
- `to summarize`
- `in summary`
- `in the realm of`
- `delve into`
- `robust`
- `crucial`
- `vital`
- `impactful`
- `leverage` as a verb
- `unlock potential`
- `synergy`
- `absolutely` as an opener
- `certainly` as an opener
- `of course` as an opener
- `fascinating`
- `intriguing`
- `exciting` when it adds no content
- `navigate` used metaphorically
- `this is a great question`
- `as previously mentioned`

---

## 3. Structural Patterns To Avoid

- em-dash-heavy rhythm
- generic three-part intro, body, conclusion structure
- transition fillers that do not carry real logic
- nested bullets used as a crutch
- excessive bolding
- heavy hedging without real uncertainty

Write for a reader who is present and paying attention.

Do not repeat the same point three times in softer language.

---

## 4. Student-Facing Content Rules

Avoid:

- `I'll help you`
- `Let me`
- `Here's how`
- `Don't worry`
- `You've got this`

Use instructor voice, not assistant voice.

Do not include time estimates in student-visible content unless the
instructor explicitly wants them.

Emojis should appear only when requested or when the existing course style
already uses them intentionally.

---

## 5. Quick Check Before Finalizing

1. Remove em-dash clusters or rewrite the sentence.
1. Remove banned phrases.
1. Replace generic claims with concrete nouns, files, dates, or actions.
1. Cut bold that is not doing real work.
1. Check whether transitions are earning their place.
