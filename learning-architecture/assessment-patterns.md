# Assessment Patterns

**Status: CANONICAL.**

This file is a cross-course catalog of assessment formats that work well in
GenAI-aware teaching environments.

Use it to vary evidence of learning across lecture, lab, and async work.

The point is not gamification for its own sake.

The point is to create short, legible checks that reveal whether students can
explain, verify, debug, transfer, or critique.

---

## 1. Quick In-Class Checks

### Poll Or Response Variants

Use lightweight polls, open-response prompts, or confidence-rated questions
to check whether students can commit to an answer in public and then defend
it.

Best for:

- conceptual checkpoints
- misconception surfacing
- discussion launch points

### One-Minute Debug

Show a short buggy code snippet, equation, argument, or workflow.

Ask students to identify the failure and propose the minimal fix.

Best for:

- programming fluency
- notation fluency
- causal reasoning

### Explain The Output

Show output, a plot, or a model response and ask students to explain what it
means without rerunning the process.

Best for:

- interpretation
- transfer
- anti-copying verification

---

## 2. Pair And Small-Group Patterns

### Relay Solution Building

One team starts a solution or explanation.

The next team must critique it, extend it, or repair it.

Best for:

- multi-step reasoning
- peer explanation
- incremental proof of understanding

### Peer Prediction

Students predict whether another team's code, argument, or prompt will pass
hidden tests or hold up under scrutiny.

Best for:

- careful review
- debugging habits
- skepticism toward first drafts

### Role-Based Deliberation

Teams receive a scenario and argue from assigned roles such as engineer,
analyst, reviewer, or policy lead.

Best for:

- ethics
- tradeoff analysis
- stakeholder reasoning

---

## 3. Puzzle And Simulation Formats

### Escape-Room Chain

Teams solve linked tasks where one correct answer unlocks the next.

Best for:

- cumulative reasoning
- station-based class sessions
- review days

### System Or Process Draft

Students assemble a workflow or architecture under explicit constraints such
as cost, latency, fairness, or failure tolerance.

Best for:

- design tradeoffs
- systems thinking
- capstone preparation

### Embodied Or Physical Simulation

Students act out an algorithm, queue, pipeline, or decision rule.

Best for:

- sequencing
- state changes
- making hidden mechanics visible

---

## 4. Creative And Interpretive Formats

### Visualization Swap

Students create a chart or table, then another team infers the question it
answers and critiques whether the design supports that question.

Best for:

- communication
- data interpretation
- audience awareness

### Human Vs AI Critique

Provide a flawed summary, chart, explanation, or code sample produced by a
model.

Ask students to diagnose what is wrong and repair it.

Best for:

- GenAI verification habits
- debugging
- close reading

### Prompt And Repair

Students generate a first draft with AI, then submit the diagnosis and repair
log rather than just the final artifact.

Best for:

- process visibility
- revision habits
- making AI use inspectable

---

## 5. Async Patterns

### Short Verification Memo

Students submit a brief explanation of what they checked, what failed, and how
they revised.

Best for:

- homework with AI assistance allowed
- reproducibility habits
- grading process rather than just output

### Compare Two Approaches

Students contrast two solutions, prompts, model outputs, or methods and argue
for one under a concrete constraint.

Best for:

- transfer
- tradeoff reasoning
- discouraging answer copying

### Annotated Artifact

Students submit code, a derivation, or a draft with short comments explaining
why the key decisions were made.

Best for:

- metacognition
- debugging history
- grading partial understanding

---

## 6. Selection Heuristics

Use shorter formats when:

- enrollment is large
- manual grading time is tight
- the goal is a quick comprehension check

Use richer formats when:

- the course needs evidence of revision
- students are using AI heavily
- tradeoffs matter more than one correct answer

Prefer patterns that make the student's checking process visible.
