# Course Design Principles

These principles apply across courses. Course-specific variations are noted
where they exist.

---

## 1. The Learning Arc

Every unit of content should move through a coherent arc:

**Explore → Consolidate → Verify**
(with optional extensions: Reinforce, Extend, Synthesize)

| Phase | What happens | Typical vehicle |
|---|---|---|
| **Explore** | First encounter with the material; productive struggle | GAIE (out of class) |
| **Consolidate** | Instructor-led; builds shared understanding from prior exposure | Lecture, live demo, pair activity |
| **Verify** | Students demonstrate understanding; gaps surface | Lab, in-class activity, assessment |
| **Reinforce** | Returns to prior skills; cumulative review | Later lectures, cumulative assessment portions |
| **Extend** | Layers new complexity onto a mastered skill | Follow-on lecture, advanced lab |
| **Synthesize** | Connects across units; builds toward a larger whole | Project, portfolio, capstone |

Design activities by asking: which phase does this serve? Every session
should fit somewhere in an arc — not just "cover the topic."

### DS-100 weekly rhythm

**GAIE (out of class) → Tuesday Consolidation → Thursday Assessment → Friday Lab**

Each component must explicitly connect:
- Tuesday activities reference specific GAIE problems
- Tuesday activities preview Thursday assessment topics
- Friday labs reinforce and extend Thursday assessment skills

---

## 2. Skill levels: I → P → A

Track each skill through Introduced, Practiced, and Assessed. See
`skill-levels.md` for the full framework.

Not every skill reaches A. Some topics are intentionally I-only or I+P.
Design your coverage deliberately, not by default.

---

## 3. Gaps are the signal

A coverage matrix or skills tracker exists to show what is MISSING, not to
celebrate what is present. Uncovered skills, unassessed competencies, and
planning gaps are the primary signals.

When a session is canceled, identify which skill lines break and where — then
decide how to recover coverage or explicitly accept the gap.

---

## 4. Cumulative assessment

All assessments should include 20–30% content from prior units.

This ensures continuous review, prevents "forgetting after the topic ends,"
and builds authentic integration of knowledge. It also reduces cramming.

---

## 5. Spine roles (session types)

Each session has a role that describes its pedagogical function. These are
defined per-course but the underlying categories are shared:

| Role | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `BRIDGE` | Transition between topics; recap + orient forward |
| `BUILD` | Hands-on; directly scaffolds an assignment or project phase |
| `PERSPECTIVE` | Conceptual/analytical; zooms out; no direct deliverable |
| `STUDIO` | Work session; students work, instructor facilitates |

Assign a role to every lecture. It clarifies purpose and prevents sessions
from being vaguely "covering material."

---

## 6. Keep manual grading low

Minimize instructor manual grading burden:
- Prefer autograded assessments where possible
- When manual grading is needed, keep responses short (1–2 paragraphs max)
- Use clear rubrics
- Use TA time for live understanding checks, not written work grading
- Vary assessment formats to reduce "just another quiz" fatigue

---

## 7. Real-world data

All assignments and examples should use real-world datasets that:
- Connect to students' lives and interests
- Raise authentic questions about society or practice
- Present ethical considerations
- Support diverse student interests

Avoid synthetic datasets or purely toy problems unless pedagogically necessary.

---

## 8. GenAI as a first-class tool

GenAI tools are integrated into the course as a learning instrument, not
prohibited as a cheat. The verification model is central: students are
expected to review, test, and modify AI-generated content, not accept it.

Design activities that make the verification habit visible and gradeable.
